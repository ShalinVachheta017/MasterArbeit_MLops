Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive sequence-to-sequence event prediction framework.
It leverages a bidirectional LSTM encoder–decoder architecture to model complex tem-
poral dependencies in variable-length sequential data. The proposed approach addresses
key challenges (including vanishing gradients, padding-related inconsistencies, and mask-
ing issues) by introducing custom evaluation metrics such as masked cross-entropy loss,
token-level accuracy, and a novel coverage metric. A systematic hyperparameter tuning
process was conducted, utilizing grid search across multiple configurations (varying batch
size, hidden size, and number of layers) combined with k-fold cross-validation. This ap-
proach enabled the selection of an optimal model that achieved a test accuracy exceeding
75%(.7563) and a test coverage of approximately 70%(.7023) on real-world datasets gen-
erated from simulations and event logs. Experimental results demonstrate the model’s
strong generalization ability and effectiveness in capturing critical event patterns, sur-
passing traditional unidirectional approaches. These findings underscore the framework’s
potential for deployment in safety-critical applications (for example autonomous systems,
cybersecurity monitoring, industrial automation). They also lay the groundwork for fu-
ture enhancements, such as incorporating advanced architectures like Transformers to
further improve performance.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Contextual Overview
Sequence modeling is a fundamental paradigm in machine learning and serves as a core
technique for applications including natural language processing, time-series forecasting,
and event detection. In safety-critical domains such as autonomous vehicles, aerospace
engineering, and industrial automation, adaptive time-triggered scheduling systems are
still emerging. Event prediction within such systems enables the pre-loading of future
schedules to achieve dynamic adaptability. These systems facilitate deterministic and syn-
chronized task execution, thereby providing significant advantages in reliability, precision,
and inherent fault tolerance. The present research aims to employ a Sequence-to-Sequence
(Seq2Seq) architecture augmented with bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
networks, in order to effectively model contextual dependencies encompassing both past
and future temporal information.
To ensure rigorous evaluation and enhance model
performance, this study integrates custom-designed evaluation metrics and employs op-
timized training strategies. These methodological enhancements include hyperparameter
tuning with k-fold cross-validation, and they have the overarching aim of addressing
existing challenges.
These challenges include the effective handling of variable-length
sequences, the mitigation of masking issues, and ensuring robust model scalability and
robust in practical applications.
1.2
Problem Statement
While sequence modeling is crucial for temporal pattern analysis, significant advance-
ments are still required to develop robust and accurate predictive models, particularly
when facing substantial challenges such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), are often
limited in their ability to effectively capture long-term dependencies due to issues like
vanishing gradients [21]. Although Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks mitigate
these limitations by including memory gates [18], their application to complex, real-world
scenarios introduces additional difficulties. A prominent challenge in sequence modeling
lies in managing datasets with variable-length sequences. These datasets frequently re-
quire padding masks to align sequence lengths and duplication masks to ignore repeated
events during evaluation. In this work, we define two masking strategies: mask (excludes
padding positions) and mask2 (excludes both padding and duplicate events).
These
strategies improve training accuracy and ensure fair evaluation of predictive performance.

However, improper handling of the masks can significantly impair model performance [3].
Moreover, sequence datasets often encompass dynamic features such as cyclic patterns
and intricate interdependencies, which are not easily modeled using conventional ap-
proaches. Additionally, architectures that process sequences in a unidirectional manner
fail to capture bidirectional dependencies, thereby limiting their capacity to fully model
the contextual relationships inherent in sequential data. While Bidirectional LSTM net-
works offer a promising solution [37], they introduce computational complexities that
necessitate careful optimization.
• Key challenges in sequence-to-sequence event prediction:
– Evaluation limitations: Conventional metrics (for example, accuracy, cross-entropy
loss) ignore masked or padded tokens, producing biased and unreliable perfor-
mance estimates.
– Hyperparameter complexity: Tuning critical parameters (hidden state
size, number of layers, bidirectionality, learning rate, etc.)
across varied
datasets is computationally intensive and error-prone.
– Contextual inadequacy: Unidirectional architectures fail to capture both
past and future dependencies inherent in sequential data.
• Proposed solution: In this thesis, we develop a mask-aware Sequence-to-Sequence
framework built on bidirectional LSTM networks, combined with systematic hyper-
parameter optimization (grid search utilizing cross-validation) to deliver reliable,
scalable event-prediction performance.
The proposed framework aims to enhance contextual understanding by leveraging bidirec-
tional dependencies. It also incorporates mask-aware custom loss and accuracy metrics to
ensure reliable training and evaluation. Furthermore, systematic hyperparameter tuning
and k-fold cross-validation techniques are employed to establish a scalable and general-
izable approach that can effectively handle the complexities inherent in diverse sequence
datasets.
1.3
Objectives of the Research
Effective sequence modeling lies at the core of many applications, such as natural lan-
guage processing [38], time-series forecasting [4], and biomedical signal analysis [35].
Despite the significant advancements achieved, existing solutions often struggle when
applied to real-world scenarios involving variable-length sequences and complex tempo-
ral dependencies.
Traditional Sequence-to-Sequence models frequently fail to capture
bidirectional relationships within sequences, which are crucial for enhancing predictive
performance. Furthermore, the absence of customized evaluation metrics for masked or
padded data undermines the reliability and accuracy of these models. This research aims
to address gaps in the fieldby utilizing bidirectional LSTMs, hyperparameter tuning, and
custom evaluation measures.This work aims to construct scalable and high-performing
Sequence-to-Sequence models for real-world applications, overcoming shortcomings in
current techniques.

The main aims of this study are as follows:
1. Develop a bidirectional LSTM-based sequence-to-sequence (Seq2Seq) architecture
that learns from both forward and backward dependencies in sequences, enhancing
the model’s contextual understanding.
2. Design and implement a custom evaluation metric that properly handles masked
and padded areas in sequences for more accurate performance assessment.
3. Apply systematic hyperparameter-tuning techniques, which utilize k-fold cross-validation,
to identify the optimal configuration and improve model robustness.
4. Validate the effectiveness of the proposed framework across diverse sequential datasets
to demonstrate its scalability and generalizability in real-world applications.

Chapter 2
Background and Literature Review
2.1
Machine Learning and Neural Networks
Machine Learning is a subfield of Artificial Intelligence that involves developing systems
which learn from data and improve their performance without explicit programming
[13]. ML algorithms recognize patterns, relationships, and structures in data to make
predictions or judgements, automating complex tasks across a wide array of domains
including natural language processing, computer vision, robotics, and healthcare [46].
2.1.1
Categories of Machine Learning
Machine Learning techniques are broadly categorized into three main types:
• Supervised Learning: In supervised learning, the algorithm is trained on labeled
data, where each input has a corresponding target output.
Common examples
include classification (for example, image recognition) and regression tasks (for
example, predicting stock prices). Algorithms such as linear regression, decision
trees, and neural networks are widely used for supervised learning [7].
• Unsupervised Learning: Unsupervised learning focuses on identifying patterns
and relationships in unlabeled data. Key techniques include clustering (for exam-
ple, K-means, hierarchical clustering) and dimensionality reduction (for example,
Principal Component Analysis - PCA, t-SNE) [20]. Applications include market
segmentation and anomaly detection.
• Reinforcement Learning (RL): In reinforcement learning, an agent learns by
interacting with an environment and receiving rewards or penalties based on its
actions. The goal is to maximize cumulative rewards over time [39]. RL is widely
applied in robotics, game AI, and autonomous systems.
2.1.2
Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs)
Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) are the foundation of modern machine learning sys-
tems, inspired by the biological structure of the brain. ANNs consist of interconnected
layers of neurons, where each neuron processes inputs, applies weights, and transmits an
output through an activation function.[11]
10

--- PAGE 12 ---
The primary components of a neural network are:
• Input Layer: Accepts raw input features.
• Hidden Layers: Perform intermediate computations and extract hierarchical rep-
resentations of the input.
• Output Layer: Produces predictions or outputs.
The training of neural networks involves adjusting the weights and biases using gradi-
ent descent and backpropagation to minimize a loss function. The evolution of neural
networks can be summarized as follows:
1. Perceptron (1958): Introduced by Rosenblatt, the perceptron is a single-layer
network capable of solving linearly separable problems [36].
2. Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs): By introducing hidden layers and non-linear
activation functions, MLPs can model complex non-linear relationships.
3. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs): CNNs, popularized by LeCun [24],
are designed for image-related tasks by capturing spatial hierarchies.
4. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs): RNNs are specialized for sequential
data, as they maintain hidden states to capture temporal dependencies [14].
5. LSTM and GRU Networks: Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated
Recurrent Unit (GRU) networks address the vanishing gradient problem, enabling
effective learning of long-term dependencies [21].
6. Transformers and Attention Mechanisms: Modern architectures such as the
Transformer utilize self-attention mechanisms to process sequences in parallel, achiev-
ing state-of-the-art results in NLP and sequential tasks [42].
Neural networks form the backbone of applications such as speech recognition, natural
language processing, and autonomous systems, with ongoing advancements like generative
models (GANs) and large-scale architectures (for example, GPT models) [40].
2.2
Sequential Data and its Challenges
Sequential data consists of ordered elements where relationships evolve over time. Un-
like static data, sequential data exhibits temporal or spatial dependencies, making it
inherently more complex to model. Such data is pervasive in real-world applications —
for example, time-series data (measurements recorded over time, including stock market
prices, sensor readings, and weather forecasts [9]); natural language data (sentences and
paragraphs where word order influences meaning, as in machine translation and senti-
ment analysis [38]); and event logs (records of events generated by systems or workflows,
where the sequence of events reveals patterns and anomalies [45]).
11

--- PAGE 13 ---
2.2.1
Challenges in Modeling Sequential Data
Modeling sequential data introduces several challenges:
1. Variable-Length Sequences: The input and output sequences often differ in
length, requiring models that can handle dynamic input (for example, Sequence-to-
Sequence architectures).
2. Long-Term Dependencies: Capturing dependencies over long sequences is diffi-
cult due to the vanishing gradient problem in traditional RNNs. LSTMs and GRUs
mitigate this issue by using gating mechanisms [21].
3. Noise and Missing Values: Sequential data, especially in real-world scenarios,
can contain noise, missing values, or irregular intervals.
4. Dynamic Patterns: Many sequential datasets (for example, financial market or
weather data) exhibit non-stationary behavior, where underlying patterns change
over time.

2.3
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)
2.3.1
Introduction to RNNs and Sequential Data
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are a class of neural networks specifically designed
for processing sequential data, where the temporal or positional order of elements is crit-
ical. Unlike feedforward networks, which process inputs independently, RNNs introduce
recurrent connections that allow the network to maintain a hidden state. This hidden
state acts as memory, capturing information from previous time steps and enabling the
network to learn temporal dependencies in tasks such as speech recognition, natural lan-
guage processing, and time-series forecasting [14]. By using the same weights across all
time steps, RNNs efficiently handle sequences of varying lengths. The structure of an
RNN can be visualized in two forms: the rolled representation and the unrolled represen-
tation. The rolled form represents the RNN as a compact model with a loop to denote
its recurrent nature. However, for understanding the flow of information over multiple

corresponds to a copy of the network.
The hidden state Ht is updated at each time step based on the input Xt and the previous

• One-to-Many: A single input generates a sequence of outputs. This configuration

puts to a sequence of outputs, which may differ in length. It is widely used in
machine translation and speech recognition [38].
2.3.3
Challenges in RNNs: Vanishing and Exploding Gradients
Despite their ability to model sequential data, traditional RNNs face significant chal-
lenges, particularly the vanishing gradient and exploding gradient problems. These issues
arise during the training process, especially when handling long sequences.The vanishing
gradient problem occurs when gradients diminish exponentially as they are propagated
backward through time during training. This phenomenon prevents earlier layers in the
sequence from receiving meaningful updates, which makes it difficult for RNNs to learn
long-term dependencies[6]. On the other hand, exploding gradients occur when gradients
grow too large in magnitude, causing unstable updates for weights and divergence dur-
ing the training. process. These challenges hinder the performance of standard RNNs,
particularly for tasks involving long-range temporal relationships.
2.3.4
Solutions to RNN Challenges
To address these limitations, several advancements have been proposed. Long Short-Term
Memory (LSTM) networks, introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [21], incorporate
a memory cell and gating mechanisms (input, forget, and output gates) that regulate
the flow of information, enabling the network to selectively remember or forget infor-
mation across long sequences.A simpler alternative is the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU),
which combines the forget and input gates into a single update gate, reducing compu-
tational complexity while maintaining performance [10]. Additionally, gradient clipping
has emerged as a practical solution to stabilize training by rescaling gradients that exceed
a predefined threshold [32].
2.3.5
Training Methodology
The training of RNNs relies on a modified version of backpropagation known as Back-
propagation Through Time (BPTT). In BPTT, the RNN is conceptually unrolled across
all time steps in a sequence, transforming it into a multi-layer feedforward network where
each layer corresponds to a specific time step. The loss function is computed at each
time step, and gradients are propagated backward through the unrolled network to up-
date weights shared across all time steps.
The key steps in BPTT are:
1. Unrolling the RNN: The recurrent structure is unfolded over the sequence length,
creating multiple time-step copies of the network.
2. Forward Pass: The network computes the hidden states and outputs sequentially
for all time steps.
14

--- PAGE 16 ---
3. Backward Pass: Gradients of the loss function are calculated for each time step and
propagated backward through the unrolled network to update the weights.
4. Weight Updates: The gradients are aggregated across all time steps, and optimiza-
tion techniques such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) are applied to update
the weights.
While BPTT enables RNNs to train on sequential data, its computational cost increases
with sequence length. Additionally, long sequences exacerbate the vanishing and explod-
ing gradient problems, necessitating the use of LSTMs, GRUs, or gradient clipping for
effective training. Despite these challenges, BPTT remains the fundamental methodology
for training RNNs and their variants, allowing them to learn complex temporal patterns.
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are fundamental models for processing sequential
data, offering the ability to learn temporal relationships and model complex sequences.
Through different input-output configurations, RNNs adapt to diverse tasks, such as clas-
sification, prediction, and sequence generation. However, traditional RNNs are limited
by vanishing and exploding gradients, particularly in long sequences. Advancements like
LSTMs, GRUs, and gradient clipping have significantly improved RNN training and per-
formance. Combined with training methodologies like Backpropagation Through Time,
these innovations enable RNNs to capture short-term and long-term dependencies, facil-

and time-series analysis.
2.4
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Networks
2.4.1
Introduction
Recurrent Neural Networks have become essential in modeling sequential data. However,
it suffers from severe limitations due to the vanishing gradient problem when learning de-
pendencies over long sequences: during backpropagation, gradients decay exponentially,
and hence it results in ineffective learning [6]. The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
network, proposed by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber in 1997 [21], resolves this by incor-
porating a number of gating mechanisms that control the flow of information. These
mechanisms allow LSTMs to filter out or retain information selectively, thus capturing
long-term dependencies effectively.
2.4.2
Long-Term Dependency Learning
In standard RNNs, gradients diminish as they are propagated backward through time.
This hampers the network’s ability to learn long-term dependencies because updates to
weights for earlier time steps become negligible. For instance, in tasks requiring a rela-
tionship between t1 and t100, standard RNNs fail to maintain meaningful gradients [6].
In contrast, gradients can grow uncontrollably during backpropagation, leading to nu-
merical instability and convergence issues. Although less common, this issue is equally
detrimental to effective training. Traditional RNNs process one time step at a time with-
out explicit mechanisms for storing or forgetting information. This limitation restricts
their ability to model sequences with long-term dependencies.
15

--- PAGE 17 ---
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks mitigate the vanishing gradient problem
by allowing gradients to flow uninterrupted through the cell state. The gating mecha-
nisms—Forget Gate, Input Gate, and Output Gate—control the addition, retention, and
removal of information, enabling the network to handle long-term dependencies effec-
tively [21]. Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) simplify the LSTM architecture by merging
the forget and input gates into a single update gate. Additionally, GRUs use a reset gate
to control the influence of past information, making them computationally efficient while
maintaining the ability to model long-term dependencies [10].
Attention mechanisms
assign dynamic weights to different time steps, enabling the model to selectively focus
on relevant portions of the sequence and significantly enhancing the ability to model
long-range dependencies [2]. Transformer models replace recurrent operations with self-
attention mechanisms, enabling parallel computation and improved scalability for long
sequences, excelling in tasks requiring global context such as language translation and
text summarization [42].

2.5
Encoder-Decoder Architectures for Sequence Mod-
eling
One of the earliest and most influential implementations of the encoder-decoder architec-
ture was proposed by Sutskever et al. [38]. In their work, LSTM networks were employed
for both the encoder and decoder, enabling successful end-to-end learning for machine
translation tasks. The model introduced a method for handling variable-length sequences
by encoding the entire input into a fixed-length context vector. Although effective, this
approach faced challenges when processing long sequences due to the limited capacity
of the context vector, which eventually motivated enhancements such as the attention
mechanism.
2.5.1
Introduction
The encoder-decoder architecture is a foundational framework in deep learning, partic-
ularly suited for sequence modeling tasks such as machine translation, time-series fore-
casting, event prediction, and speech recognition. Introduced by Sutskever et al. [38] for
neural machine translation, this architecture has since been adapted for various domains
requiring input-output sequences of differing lengths. It transforms a variable-length in-
put sequence X = [x1, x2, . . . , xT] into a corresponding variable-length output sequence
Y = [y1, y2, . . . , yT ′] through an intermediate context vector C.

In this architecture, the encoder processes the input sequence into a fixed-length hidden
representation (context vector), while the decoder uses this context to sequentially gener-

To facilitate learning, input tokens are typically converted to dense vector representations

2.5.4
Attention Mechanism
The original encoder-decoder model used a single fixed-length context vector C to repre-
sent the entire input sequence, which limited its ability to capture long-term dependencies
or complex structures. Bahdanau et al. [2] addressed this limitation by introducing the
attention mechanism, which enables the decoder to directly access all encoder hidden
states and compute context vectors dynamically. At each decoding step, the decoder
assigns alignment weights to encoder states based on their relevance, allowing the model
to focus on the most informative parts of the input. This innovation significantly im-
proved performance in neural machine translation and laid the groundwork for subsequent
attention-based architectures.

2.5.6
Architectural Variants
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are the most basic architecture for sequence model-
ing. RNNs maintain a hidden state that carries forward sequential information, allowing
the model to process temporal dependencies. However, they suffer from the vanishing
gradient problem when dealing with long sequences, making it difficult to learn long-term
dependencies [6].
To address this, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [21] and Gated Recurrent
Units (GRUs) [10] were introduced. These architectures use gating mechanisms—such
as input, forget, and output gates in LSTMs—to control the flow of information and
retain relevant features over longer time horizons. GRUs offer a simpler architecture while
achieving comparable performance in many cases. Bidirectional RNNs[37] enhance model
performance by processing the input sequence in both forward and backward directions.
This bi-directionality is particularly useful in tasks like named entity recognition and
sentiment analysis, where future context provides valuable information. However, BRNNs
are not always suitable for causal tasks like event prediction where future data is not
accessible at inference time.

Applications
Encoder-decoder models have enabled breakthroughs across multiple domains. In neural
machine translation, Sequence-to-Sequence models using LSTMs and later Transformers

2.5.9
Challenges and Limitations
Despite their advantages, encoder-decoder models present several challenges. Transformer
models, in particular, are computationally expensive due to their multi-head attention
mechanisms and high memory consumption. Large-scale training requires extensive la-
beled datasets, making it difficult to apply these models in low-resource or domain-specific
scenarios. Additionally, overfitting is a persistent concern, especially when training data
is limited. Regularization techniques such as dropout, early stopping, and data augmen-
tation are essential to mitigate these issues [42].
